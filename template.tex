\begin{abstract}

Perceptual biases along the schizotypy spectrum affect causal inference during physics-altered events, yet the
mechanisms underlying these differences remain unclear. This study investigated how schizotypal traits relate to
intuitive physics reasoning by examining susceptibility to stimuli with artificial physics through subsequent predictions. Online participants predicted the final position of a target ball following collision, after which the target became occluded. Physics were systematically violated through angular egress deviations (±15°), spatial overlaps (0.75-1.25 contact ratios), temporal asynchronies (1-5 frame delays), and size-mass mismatches. Participants completed the Cardiff Anomalous Perceptual Experiences Scale (CAPS), Peters Delusion Inventory (PDI), and Schizotypal Personality Questionnaire (SPQ) to assess individual differences in psychotic-like experiences. Results revealed systematic biases toward physics-violating endpoints at large angular deviations (mean errors of -2 to -6 units), suggesting reliance on perceptual heuristics rather than mental simulation. Mouse tracking metrics identified two distinct decision styles: "Trackers" exhibited fast, ballistic responses but higher prediction error, while "Waiters" showed deliberative movements with greater accuracy. Higher schizotypy scores (CAPS, PDI, SPQ) predicted slower movement velocities and 18-35 but did not significantly impair prediction accuracy. These findings suggest that schizotypal traits primarily affect motor execution and decision confidence rather than the underlying intuitive physics reasoning. The results support dual-process models of causal perception and highlight the value of mouse tracking for dissecting cognitive phenotypes in individual differences research.

\end{abstract}

To do so, we draw inspiration for a perception task using principles of object tracking and state estimation. It involves tracking a ball and predicting the endpoint of its trajectory after a possible collision with another ball. Like previous versions of the task, each trial has the potential of an artificial condition manipulating the "true" trajectory of two balls. Artificial egress can affect the angle of the second balls angular velocity after a collision. In another condition, the intuitive association that  heavier things are bigger is challenged as mass and size are disassociated, causing visible changes to their physics after collisions. Several conditions alter the perceptual difficulty of the task, but not the movement trajectory, such as the the overlap allowed between balls before collision, and the temporal delay that follows post-collision.

Probabilistic simulation models assume that humans can use a mental model about how systems interact to simulate possible future states \citep{hohwy_predictive_2013}. Noisy Newtonian models utilize a structure in accordance to physical constraints to assess the influence of noise on individual perception \citep{kubricht_intuitive_2017}. Previous models have characterized noise in the generative physics process into dynamics and perceptual uncertainty \citep{smith_sources_2013}. Active inference models build on Bayesian inference models to incorporate beliefs (or preferences) about the likelihood of future trajectories \citep{parr_active_2022}. Since the model space of potential trajectories is often intractable, we constrain our model to two possible trajectories- the "physics-aligned" trajectory, which is what Newtonian physics would predict after object collisions, and the "physics-violating" trajectory, which is tied to the artificial physics that actually govern the movement of the visual stimuli. Models which compare or include these trajectory calculations will be compared.  

%introduce sz eye movement problems and how it may link to tracking
Unexplored is the possibility of counterfactual object trajectories simulated and weighted in perception \citep{gerstenberg_counterfactual_2024}. The tracking of multiple trajectories of moving objects naturally builds upon intuitions of counterfactual reasoning. In similar object collision tasks, previous models have probed what would have happened had a condition been absent \citep{gerstenberg_eye-tracking_2017}. Through a continuous endpoint prediction, we assess if there is an influence of counterfactual simulation that affects decision-making. To this end, we assess under what (if any) conditions bias predictions towards or away from alternative trajectories. Similar active inference models have been previously used to describe eye dynamics in schizophrenia \citep{adams_active_2015, adams_computational_2013}.

Here, we probe the susceptibility to sensory stimuli that challenge intuitive assumptions found in real-world (Newtonian) physics. Physics-artificial balls are shown to collide before an occlusion, then participants predict the end position of one ball and their confidence of causality between the two. We model this prediction task through an active inference model structured to replicate 2-d physics, for which parameters reflect different forms of uncertainty in the underlying physics \citep{smith_sources_2013}. 

\section{Methods}



\subsection{Scales}


% The Sensory Perception Quotient assesses basic sensory sensitivity along the five modalities \citep{tavassoli_sensory_2014}.

The Schizotypal Personality Questionnaire- Brief Revised Updated (SPQ-BRU) uses 32 items which cover domains used in the diagnostic criteria for Schizotypal Personality Disorder \citep{davidson_schizotypal_2016}. It can be comprised into positive, negative and disorganized ordinate factors, as well as 7 subordinate factors reflecting schizotypal personality traits. Items are rated on a 5-point Likert scale from "strongly disagree" to "strongly agree," with higher scores indicating greater schizotypy.

The Peters Delusions Inventory (PDI-21) consists of 21 items measuring delusional ideation in the general population \citep{peters_measuring_2004}. Each item assesses the presence of a specific delusional belief (yes/no), with endorsed items further rated on three dimensions: distress, preoccupation, and conviction (1-5 Likert scale). Based on the Present State Examination, the scale encompasses eight categories of delusions including persecution, reference, control, and thought interference. It is designed to assess the continuum between normal and delusional thinking, with the multidimensional ratings distinguishing psychosis-prone from psychotic individuals.

The Cardiff Anomalous Perceptual Experiences Scale (CAPS) consists of 32 items assessing anomalous perceptual experiences across all sensory modalities, including auditory, visual, olfactory, gustatory, tactile, and temporal perception \citep{bell_cardiff_2006}. Each endorsed item is rated on three subscales measuring distress, intrusiveness, and frequency (1-5 Likert scale). It is used to measure perceptual anomalies across the psychosis continuum in both clinical and non-clinical populations.

To identify distinct psychometric subtypes, we performed k-means clustering on standardized subscale scores. We first computed all 12 subscales (4 SPQ + 4 PDI + 4 CAPS) for each participant and standardized them to z-scores. We then applied k-means clustering with k ranging from 2 to 6 and selected the optimal number of clusters using the silhouette method. The k-means algorithm was run with 50 random initializations to ensure stable solutions. Cluster stability was assessed via bootstrapping (1000 iterations). 

\subsection{Physics task}

32 participants (with normal or corrected vision, right-handed) participated in the online experiment, drawn from a pool of University of Technology Sydney undergraduates who completed for course credit and via Prolific. The scales and experiment were adapted for usage via Psychopy [version number] and uploaded on Pavlovia. Ethics was approved under the University of Technology Sydney Psychology Low and Negligible Risks, approval number [G–60–2024-12-10].  Participants were given a short training instructions on the structure of the task, and asked to confirm understanding of the task. Participants were excluded if they: (1) failed to complete all experimental blocks, (2) showed response times <100ms or >30s on >20\% of trials, or (3) showed non-engagement patterns (e.g., clicking in same location repeatedly). Of the initial sample, 29 participants met all quality criteria (2,335 trials total).

Participants completed a two-ball collision prediction task consisting of 74 trials. The trial began with a mouse click of the red ball, followed by a trajectory resulting in collision of the two balls. The ball starting position was block dependent. In the michotte configuration, ball A (the "launcher") originated from a fixed launch position at the bottom of the screen (0, 15 or 30 degrees off center) toward Ball B (the "target"), which remained stationary at the center of the screen. Following collision, Ball B moved toward a catch zone at the top of the screen while Ball A continued along its post-collision trajectory. This configuration replicated classic launching effect paradigms. On the other block, both balls started at the bottom position, and began with trajectories to collide near the center of screen. Both balls became occluded halfway between the collision point and the catch zone, requiring participants to mentally extrapolate the target ball's final position. Ball kinematics followed constant-velocity dynamics with realistic elastic collision mechanics incorporating friction and momentum transfer, but excluded acceleration, drag, and gravity to simplify the mental physics computation. Trials were randomized with velocities [1.3, 1.7, 2.3], representing [fps]. Participants used the mouse cursor to indicate their prediction of where Ball B would land along the horizontal catch zone. Reaction time and mouse cursor coordinates were recorded at 60Hz trajectories were recorded continuously throughout each trial. After submitting their prediction, participants responded to the question "Do you believe the red ball caused the movement of the blue ball?" on a 5-point Likert scale ranging from "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree." This assessed explicit causal perception under physics violations. The experiment employed a full factorial design manipulating five physics parameters across 74 trials:

Control trials (12 total): Six trials with veridical Newtonian physics preceded the experiment, and six control trials concluded the session to assess baseline performance and learning effects. Importantly, these trials pre-collision occlusion trials (where occlusion occurred halfway between trial onset and collision). 

Spatial overlap (10 trials): The degree of spatial contact at collision was varied across five levels (0.75, 0.9, 1.0, 1.1, 1.25), where 1.0 represents naturalistic edge-to-edge contact. Values below 1.0 created apparent "underlap" (balls separated at collision), while values above 1.0 produced visible interpenetration. This manipulation tested sensitivity to anomalous spatial contact between objects.

Angular egress (14 trials): The post-collision trajectory angle of Ball B was artificially deviated from the physically accurate angle by -15°, -7.5°, -4.5°, 0°, +4.5°, +7.5°, or +15°. In naturalistic elastic collisions between equal-mass spheres, the angle between post-collision trajectories is constrained by conservation of momentum. Egress deviations violated this constraint, creating a discrepancy between the perceptually expected and
physically correct endpoints. This manipulation assessed reliance on angle heuristics versus mental simulation.

Temporal asynchrony (10 trials): The onset of Ball B's movement was delayed by 1-5 frames (16.7-83.3 ms at 60 Hz) following Ball A's contact, creating apparent violations of temporal contiguity in causal interactions. This tested whether participants required immediate temporal contingency for causal attribution.

Size-mass decoupling (18 trials): Ball B's visual size (diameter: 0.71×, 1.0×, or 1.3× the base 0.125 units) was manipulated independently of its mass (0.6×, 1.0×, or 1.4× the base value) in a 3×3 factorial design. Post-collision trajectories reflected the true mass rather than the visible size, violating the natural size-mass correlation. This assessed whether participants inferred mass from visual size during collision prediction.

Interaction conditions (16 trials): Eight trials combined overlap × egress manipulations, and eight combined temporal × egress manipulations to test non-additive effects.

\subsection{Metrics}

For each trial, two simulation trajectories were computed: one using the manipulated physics parameters (physics-violating, PV) and one using veridical Newtonian mechanics (physics-aligned, PA). The horizontal landing positions along the catch zone for these two simulations defined the PV and PA endpoints, respectively. Participant predictions were translated into to a signed directional error, normalized by the distance from the PA
endpoint toward the PV endpoint. Error ranged from 0 (perfectly aligned with physics) to +1 (perfectly aligned with the violation), with larger values representing overshoot towards each endpoint, respectively. 

The dynamics of both "physics-aligned" and "physics-violating" trajectories were simulated for each trial, given the starting positions of each ball. Velocity varied randomly on each trial, between x and y, resulting in trials with x-y frames and x-y ms. To assess how physics violations affected prediction accuracy, we conducted mixed-effects linear regression models predicting absolute error from each manipulation dimension. Models included manipulation level as a fixed effect and participant as a random intercept to account for repeated measures. Models were fit using maximum likelihood estimation (Python statsmodels). We report regression coefficients ($\beta$), standard errors, and $p$-values. Statistical significance was evaluated at $\alpha$ = .05 with Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons (5 manipulations: $\alpha_{\text{corrected}}$ = .01).


Mouse trajectories were extracted from movement initiation to final click. For each trial, we computed: (1) trajectory curvature (area between actual and straight-line path), (2) maximum perpendicular deviation, (3) number of direction reversals, (4) initiation time, and (5) total movement duration. To characterize individual decision styles, we performed k-means clustering on standardized trial-averaged mouse metrics, identifying ``direct responders'' (straight, fast trajectories that "track" the objects movement) and ``deliberative responders'' (slower trajectories with more deliberation time). We then tested whether decision style was associated with psychometric profiles using logistic regression, and whether it predicted performance using mixed-effects models.

\subsection{Active Inference for Object Tracking}

The environment provides Cartesian coordinates $[X_a, Y_a, X_b, Y_b]$ for each object, which are transformed into polar coordinates $(\theta, r)$ where $\theta \in [-1, 1]$ represents the angle and $r \in [0, \infty)$ represents the radial distance from origin. Cartesian velocities $[v_x, v_y]$ are similarly transformed to angular and radial velocities $(\omega, v_r)$:

\begin{align}
\theta &= \arctan2(y, x) \cdot \frac{180}{\pi}, \quad r = \sqrt{dx^2 + dy^2} \\
v_r &= \frac{dx \cdot v_x + dy \cdot v_y}{r}, \quad \omega = \frac{dx \cdot v_y - dy \cdot v_x}{r^2}
\end{align}

Agents maintain beliefs over angular position and velocity in generalized coordinates $u_\theta = [u_\theta^{(0)}, u_\theta^{(1)}] \in [-1, 1]^2$. The dynamics function models attractor dynamics with strength $\lambda$ pulling beliefs toward target $v$.

\begin{equation}
\text{polarTranslation}([x,y]) = (\theta, r) = \left[\arctan2(y,x) \cdot \frac{180}{\pi}, \sqrt{dx^2 + dy^2}\right]
\label{eq:polar_transform}
\end{equation}

Cartesian velocity $[v_x, v_y]$ is also translated to angular and radial velocity $(\omega, v_r)$. Radial velocity is the component along the radial vector:
\begin{equation}
v_r = \frac{dx \cdot v_x + dy \cdot v_y}{r}
\end{equation}

Angular velocity is along the perpendicular component:
\begin{equation}
\omega = \frac{dx \cdot v_y - dy \cdot v_x}{r^2}
\end{equation}

The agents represent beliefs about angular position and angular velocity in generalized coordinates $u_\theta = [u_\theta^{(0)}, u_\theta^{(1)}]$, normalized to $[-1,1]$.

The dynamics function designates the strength of attractor dynamics, or the pull of beliefs towards the target:
\begin{equation}
e_i = (v - x) \cdot \lambda
\end{equation}

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Active Inference Update Step}
\label{alg:active_inference}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Current state $x = [x^{(0)}, x^{(1)}]$, target $v$, observation $o$, action $a_t$
\STATE \textbf{Parameters:} Precisions $\Pi_{\eta_x}, \Pi_x, \Pi_{\eta_v}, \Pi_o$; learning rate $\text{lr}_a$; timestep $dt$
\STATE 
\STATE \textit{Step 1: Compute prior prediction error}
\STATE $\epsilon_{\eta_x} \gets \Pi_{\eta_x} (x^{(0)} - \eta_x)$
\STATE 
\STATE \textit{Step 2: Dynamics prediction and error}
\STATE $x' \gets f(x^{(0)}, v) = \lambda (v - x^{(0)})$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Predict next state
\STATE $\epsilon_x \gets \Pi_x (x^{(1)} - x')$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Dynamics prediction error
\STATE $\epsilon_{\eta_v} \gets \Pi_{\eta_v} (v - \eta_v)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Cause prediction error
\STATE 
\STATE \textit{Step 3: Observation prediction}
\STATE $o' \gets g(x)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Forward generative model
\STATE $\epsilon_o \gets \Pi_o (o - o')$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Observation prediction error
\STATE 
\STATE \textit{Step 4: Compute free energy gradients}
\STATE $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} \gets \frac{\partial \epsilon_o}{\partial x} + \epsilon_{\eta_x} + \frac{\partial \epsilon_x}{\partial x}$
\STATE $\frac{\partial F}{\partial v} \gets \epsilon_{\eta_v} + \frac{\partial \epsilon_x}{\partial v}$
\STATE 
\STATE \textit{Step 5: Update beliefs via gradient descent on free energy}
\STATE $\dot{x} \gets Dx - \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}$ \hfill $\triangleright$ $D$ is shift operator: $D[x^{(0)}, x^{(1)}] = [x^{(1)}, 0]$
\STATE $\dot{v} \gets -\frac{\partial F}{\partial v}$
\STATE $x \gets x + dt \cdot \dot{x}$
\STATE $v \gets v + dt \cdot \dot{v}$
\STATE 
\STATE \textit{Step 6: Update action to minimize observation prediction error}
\STATE $\dot{a} \gets -\Pi_o \epsilon_o$
\STATE $a_{t+1} \gets a_t + dt \cdot \dot{a} \cdot \text{lr}_a$
\STATE 
\STATE \textbf{Return:} Updated state $x$, target $v$, action $a_{t+1}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Agent Architectures}

We evaluated three active inference agents differing in their generative models and learning mechanisms:

\subsubsection{Agent 2.1: Single Learned Attractor}

\paragraph{Generative Model:}

Agent 2.1 employs a single-context generative model with endpoint attractors:

\begin{align*}
\text{State space: } & x = [x_\theta, \dot{x}_\theta]^T  \\
\text{Hidden causes: } & v = \eta_v \in \mathbb{R} \text{ ( target endpoint)}
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Dynamics Equation:}

$$
f(x, v) = \lambda (v - x_\theta)
$$

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda$: Attractor strength parameter (action precision)
    \item $v$: endpoint 
    \item $x_\theta$: Current angle position
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Observation Model:}
Proprioceptive observation (position):
$$
g_{\text{prop}}(x) = x_\theta
$$

\subsubsection{Agent 2.2: Velocity-Based Dynamics}

\paragraph{Generative Model:}

Agent 2.2 uses velocity as a hidden cause that directly controls dynamics:

\begin{align*}
\text{State space: } & x = [x_\theta, \dot{x}_\theta]^T  \\
\text{Hidden causes: } & v = \text{velocity\_cause} 
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Dynamics Equation:}

$$
f(x, v) = \lambda \cdot v
$$

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\lambda$: Action precision
    \item $v$: Velocity cause (learned)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Observation Models:}

In addition to the previous observation, this model now includes: 
Visual (velocity):
$$
g_{\text{vis}}(x) = \dot{x}_\theta
$$
\subsubsection{Agent 2.4: Policy-Weighted Dual Attractors}

\paragraph{Generative Model:}

Agent 2.4 represents dual physics hypotheses (violating vs. aligned endpoints) with learnable policy weights:

\begin{align*}
\text{State space: } & x = [x_\theta, \dot{x}_\theta]^T  \\
\text{Hidden causes: } & v = [v_{\text{violating}}, v_{\text{aligned}}]^T  \text{ (policy weights)} \\
\text{Physics priors: } & \theta_{\text{violating}}, \theta_{\text{aligned}} \text{ (trial-specific endpoints)}
\end{align*}

\paragraph{Dynamics Equation:}

$$
f(x, v, \theta_{\text{viol}}, \theta_{\text{align}}) = v_0 \cdot \lambda(\theta_{\text{viol}} - x_\theta) + v_1 \cdot \lambda(\theta_{\text{align}} - x_\theta)
$$

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $v = [v_0, v_1]$: Policy weights for dual attractors (sum $\approx$ 1)
    \item $\theta_{\text{viol}}, \theta_{\text{align}}$: Trial-specific physics endpoints (extracted from collision data)
    \item $\lambda$: Attractor strength
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Analysis 2: Main Effects of Physics Manipulations}
\paragraph{Overall Performance}

Across all 2,335 trials, participants showed small directional prediction errors ($M_{\text{error from physics}}$ = 0.035, $SD$ = 0.265 normalized units), indicating minimal systematic bias toward physics-aligned or physics-violating endpoints on average. Participants reported mid-range confidence ($M$ = 4.11, $SD$ = 1.22 on 0--100 scale) with moderate response times ($M$ = 1.92s, $SD$ = 0.93s).

Performance metrics varied between control and manipulation conditions. Control trials with natural physics (60 trials) showed: directional error ($M$ = 0.031, $SD$ = 0.201), confidence ($M$ = 3.85, $SD$ = 1.34), and response time ($M$ = 2.01s, $SD$ = 0.85s). Notably, manipulation trials showed slightly higher confidence despite greater error variability, and were responded to marginally faster than control trials.

\subsection{Analysis 4: Mouse Trajectory Analysis and Decision Styles}

\paragraph{Mouse Trajectory Patterns}

Substantial individual differences emerged in mouse trajectories. Trajectory curvature ranged from near-zero (direct paths) to highly curved paths with multiple direction reversals. Curvature correlated with total movement duration ($r$ = .65) and with number of reversals ($r$ = .58), suggesting a coherent ``deliberative'' behavioral style.

\paragraph{Directional Bias in Mouse Trajectories}

To characterize how participants' mouse movements tracked the physics manipulations, we analyzed directional bias separately for pre-collision movement (before object contact) and post-collision movement (after object separation). Directional bias was computed as the signed angle between the mouse position vector and the physics-aligned endpoint vector (Figure~\ref{fig:mouse_bias_egress}).

Mouse trajectories showed systematic tracking of egress angle manipulations. For negative egress angles (leftward violations), participants showed negative directional bias (toward physics-aligned endpoints), while for positive egress angles (rightward violations), participants showed positive directional bias (toward physics-violating endpoints). Importantly, post-collision movements showed substantially stronger directional bias than pre-collision movements, particularly for larger egress angles ($\pm$7.5\textdegree{} and $\pm$15\textdegree).

\paragraph{Decision Style Classification}

K-means clustering on mouse metrics identified two primary decision styles:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Direct responders} (60\% of participants): Short, straight trajectories with few reversals
    \item \textit{Deliberative responders} (40\% of participants): Longer, curved trajectories with multiple corrections
\end{itemize}

Decision style was associated with psychometric profiles: individuals in Cluster 1 (high psychometric scores) were more likely to show deliberative response patterns (logistic regression: OR = 2.8, $p$ = .048).

\paragraph{Decision Style and Performance}

Direct responders showed slightly higher confidence ratings ($M$ = 4.3 vs. 3.9, $t$(27) = 2.1, $p$ = .045) but did not differ significantly in prediction accuracy. Deliberative responders showed stronger error-confidence correlations, suggesting more calibrated metacognitive monitoring despite lower overall confidence.

\section{Discussion}

Initial pilot results indicate that there may not be a schizotypy driven effect on performance, but instead only on confidence about their beliefs of intuitive physical interactions. There may be differences in mouse pursuit of decisions, as indicated by summary statistics. The present data show the current pipeline of analyses, and planned comparisons, but with an incomplete data set. Currently, the results on scales show only the very low end of the spectrum.
